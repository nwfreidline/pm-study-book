# Servant Leadership & Leadership Styles

## Overview
Leadership is the #1 tested concept in the People domain. PMI has a clear preference: **servant leadership** is the default correct answer unless the scenario specifically calls for something else. This chapter covers what servant leadership means, how it compares to other styles, and how to identify the right approach in exam scenarios.

---

## What Is Servant Leadership?

Servant leadership is a philosophy where the leader's primary role is to **serve the team** — removing obstacles, providing resources, shielding from organizational politics, and creating an environment where team members can do their best work.

### Core Principle
> "The servant-leader is servant first. It begins with the natural feeling that one wants to serve, to serve first." — Robert K. Greenleaf

### Key Behaviors of a Servant Leader
| Behavior | Description | Example |
|----------|-------------|--------|
| **Listening** | Actively seeks to understand team needs | Holding regular 1-on-1s to understand blockers |
| **Empathy** | Understands and shares feelings of others | Recognizing when a team member is overwhelmed |
| **Healing** | Helps team recover from setbacks | Facilitating a retrospective after a failed sprint |
| **Awareness** | Self-aware and situationally aware | Noticing team morale dropping before it becomes a crisis |
| **Persuasion** | Influences through reasoning, not authority | Building consensus rather than issuing mandates |
| **Conceptualization** | Thinks beyond day-to-day | Helping team see how their work connects to organizational strategy |
| **Foresight** | Anticipates consequences | Identifying risks before they materialize |
| **Stewardship** | Holds resources in trust for others | Protecting team's time from unnecessary meetings |
| **Commitment to growth** | Develops people | Mentoring junior team members, providing stretch assignments |
| **Building community** | Creates belonging | Fostering team identity and shared purpose |

### What Servant Leadership Is NOT
- ❌ Being passive or letting the team do whatever they want
- ❌ Avoiding difficult conversations
- ❌ Never making decisions
- ❌ Being a "pushover"
- ❌ Laissez-faire leadership (that's a different style)

🎯 **Exam Tip:** Servant leadership is ACTIVE, not passive. The servant leader actively removes impediments, actively coaches, and actively creates the right environment. If an answer choice says "let the team figure it out" — that's laissez-faire, not servant leadership.

## Leadership Styles Comparison

### 1. Servant Leadership (PMI's Preferred Default)
- **Focus:** Team's needs and growth
- **Power source:** Influence, trust, expertise
- **PM's role:** Facilitator, coach, obstacle remover
- **Best for:** Self-organizing teams, agile environments, knowledge work
- **PMI context:** Default answer for "what should the PM do?"

### 2. Transformational Leadership
- **Focus:** Inspiring change and vision
- **Power source:** Charisma, intellectual stimulation
- **PM's role:** Visionary, motivator, change agent
- **Best for:** Organizational change, innovation projects, low morale situations
- **PMI context:** Second-best answer; appropriate when team needs inspiration

### 3. Transactional Leadership
- **Focus:** Exchange — rewards for performance
- **Power source:** Formal authority, reward/punishment
- **PM's role:** Manager, performance monitor
- **Best for:** Routine work, clear deliverables, contractor management
- **PMI context:** Acceptable for specific situations but not the default

### 4. Laissez-Faire Leadership
- **Focus:** Minimal intervention
- **Power source:** Delegation (abdication)
- **PM's role:** Available if needed, otherwise hands-off
- **Best for:** Highly experienced, self-motivated expert teams
- **PMI context:** Rarely the correct answer; often a trap option

### 5. Directive (Autocratic) Leadership
- **Focus:** Command and control
- **Power source:** Positional authority
- **PM's role:** Decision-maker, order-giver
- **Best for:** Crisis situations, safety emergencies, very inexperienced teams
- **PMI context:** Only correct in emergencies or when safety is at risk

### Comparison Table

| Style | Decision Making | Team Autonomy | When PMI Wants It |
|-------|----------------|---------------|-------------------|
| Servant | Facilitated/team-driven | High | Default — most scenarios |
| Transformational | Inspired/shared vision | Medium-High | Change initiatives, vision-setting |
| Transactional | Leader decides, rewards follow | Medium | Clear deliverables, contracts |
| Laissez-Faire | Team decides independently | Very High | Almost never on exam |
| Directive | Leader decides alone | Low | Crisis/emergency only |

🎯 **Exam Tip:** When you see a question about leadership and no specific crisis or emergency is mentioned, servant leadership behaviors are almost always the correct answer. Look for answer choices that involve: facilitating, coaching, removing obstacles, empowering, or asking the team.

## Situational Leadership (Hersey & Blanchard)

PMI also recognizes that effective leaders adapt their style based on the team's maturity level:

| Team Maturity | Leadership Approach | PM Behavior |
|---------------|--------------------|-----------|
| **M1 — Low competence, low commitment** | Directing (Telling) | Provide specific instructions, close supervision |
| **M2 — Some competence, variable commitment** | Coaching (Selling) | Explain decisions, provide opportunity for clarification |
| **M3 — High competence, variable commitment** | Supporting (Participating) | Share ideas, facilitate decision-making |
| **M4 — High competence, high commitment** | Delegating | Turn over responsibility for decisions |

🎯 **Exam Tip:** If a question describes a new team member who is eager but inexperienced, the PM should use a directing/coaching approach. If the team is experienced and self-motivated, delegate. This is NOT contradicting servant leadership — a servant leader adapts their approach to what the team needs.

## Power Types in Project Management

Understanding power helps you understand why servant leadership works:

| Power Type | Source | Example | Effectiveness |
|-----------|--------|---------|---------------|
| **Expert** | Knowledge/skills | "I've done this before, here's what works" | High — earned respect |
| **Referent** | Personal relationships | Team follows you because they like/respect you | High — builds loyalty |
| **Legitimate** | Position/title | "I'm the PM, so..." | Medium — limited buy-in |
| **Reward** | Ability to give rewards | Bonuses, recognition, good assignments | Medium — transactional |
| **Coercive** | Ability to punish | Threats, bad reviews, removal from team | Low — breeds resentment |
| **Informational** | Access to information | Controlling what info flows to whom | Low-Medium — manipulative |

### PMI's Preference Order for Power
1. **Expert + Referent** (best — earned through competence and relationships)
2. **Legitimate + Reward** (acceptable — comes with the role)
3. **Coercive** (worst — almost never appropriate)

🎯 **Exam Tip:** PMI wants PMs to influence through expertise and relationships, not through positional authority or threats. If an answer choice involves "telling" the team or "escalating to their functional manager," it's usually wrong unless all collaborative approaches have failed.

## Servant Leadership in Agile vs. Predictive

### In Agile/Scrum
- The Scrum Master IS a servant leader by definition
- Removes impediments for the development team
- Coaches the Product Owner on backlog management
- Shields team from external interference
- Does NOT assign work (team self-organizes)

### In Predictive/Waterfall
- PM still applies servant leadership principles
- Removes organizational barriers
- Ensures team has resources and clear requirements
- May need to be more directive on process (but still collaborative on solutions)
- Balances serving the team with meeting stakeholder expectations

🎯 **Exam Tip:** On the PMP exam, approximately 50% of questions are agile/hybrid. In agile questions, the servant leader answer is almost always correct. In predictive questions, servant leadership is still preferred but may be balanced with more structure.

## Practice Scenarios

### Scenario 1
**A team member comes to you frustrated that another department is blocking their access to a testing environment. What do you do?**

- ❌ A) Tell the team member to escalate to their functional manager
- ❌ B) Send an email to the blocking department demanding access
- ✅ C) Work with the blocking department to understand the constraint and find a solution
- ❌ D) Wait for the issue to resolve itself

**Why C?** Servant leadership = removing obstacles. You actively intervene to help, but through collaboration, not authority.

### Scenario 2
**Your agile team is experienced and has been delivering consistently for 6 sprints. They ask if they can modify the sprint retrospective format. What do you do?**

- ❌ A) Tell them the retrospective format is defined in the process and cannot change
- ❌ B) Escalate to the Agile Coach for a decision
- ✅ C) Encourage the team to experiment with the new format and evaluate the results
- ❌ D) Modify the format yourself and present it to the team

**Why C?** Servant leadership with a mature team = empower them to self-organize and experiment.

### Scenario 3
**A critical production issue occurs at 2 AM. The team is confused about what to do first. What leadership style is appropriate?**

- ❌ A) Servant — ask the team what they think should happen
- ❌ B) Laissez-faire — let the experts handle it
- ✅ C) Directive — provide clear instructions on immediate priorities
- ❌ D) Transformational — inspire the team with a vision of the fixed system

**Why C?** Crisis situations are the ONE time directive leadership is appropriate. Safety and urgency override the default servant approach.

## Key Takeaways for the Exam

1. **Default to servant leadership** unless the scenario describes a crisis or emergency
2. **Servant leadership is active** — removing obstacles, coaching, facilitating
3. **Expert and referent power** are PMI's preferred influence methods
4. **Adapt your style** to team maturity (Situational Leadership)
5. **Transformational leadership** is appropriate for change and vision-setting
6. **Directive leadership** is only for emergencies
7. **In agile**, the servant leader role is even more pronounced (Scrum Master)
8. **Never use coercive power** unless it's literally the only option left (and even then, probably wrong on the exam)

🎯 **Final Exam Tip:** When stuck between two answers, ask yourself: "Which answer shows the PM serving the team rather than commanding the team?" That's usually the PMI-preferred answer.
