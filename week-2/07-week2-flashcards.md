## Servant Leadership & Leadership Styles

**Q:** What is PMI's preferred leadership style for project managers?

**A:** Servant leadership — the PM serves the team by removing obstacles, providing resources, coaching, and creating an environment where team members can do their best work.

---

**Q:** What are the key behaviors of a servant leader? (Name at least 5)

**A:** Listening, empathy, healing, awareness, persuasion, conceptualization, foresight, stewardship, commitment to growth of people, and building community.

---

**Q:** When is directive (autocratic) leadership appropriate?

**A:** Only in crisis/emergency situations where safety is at risk or immediate action is required and there's no time for collaboration.

---

**Q:** What is the difference between servant leadership and laissez-faire leadership?

**A:** Servant leadership is ACTIVE (removing obstacles, coaching, facilitating). Laissez-faire is PASSIVE (hands-off, minimal intervention). Servant leaders are engaged; laissez-faire leaders are absent.

---

**Q:** What are PMI's two preferred types of power?

**A:** Expert power (based on knowledge/skills) and Referent power (based on personal relationships and respect). These are earned, not positional.

---

**Q:** In Situational Leadership, what approach should you use with a new, inexperienced team member?

**A:** Directing/Telling (M1) — provide specific instructions and close supervision. As they develop competence and commitment, transition to coaching, supporting, then delegating.

---

**Q:** What is transformational leadership and when is it appropriate?

**A:** Transformational leadership inspires change through vision, charisma, and intellectual stimulation. Appropriate for organizational change initiatives, innovation projects, or when the team needs inspiration and motivation.

## Conflict Resolution

**Q:** What are the 5 Thomas-Kilmann conflict resolution techniques?

**A:** 1) Collaborate/Problem-Solve, 2) Compromise/Reconcile, 3) Smooth/Accommodate, 4) Force/Direct, 5) Withdraw/Avoid.

---

**Q:** What is PMI's preferred conflict resolution technique?

**A:** Collaborate/Problem-Solve (Win-Win) — work together to find a solution that fully satisfies all parties. This should always be tried FIRST.

---

**Q:** What is the outcome of compromise according to PMI?

**A:** Lose-Lose — both parties give up something. It's acceptable when collaboration fails or time is limited, but it's NOT the first choice because nobody gets everything they want.

---

**Q:** When is forcing/directing an appropriate conflict resolution technique?

**A:** Only in emergencies, safety situations, or as a last resort when all collaborative approaches have failed and a decision must be made immediately.

---

**Q:** What is the difference between withdrawing and smoothing?

**A:** Withdrawing = retreating from the conflict entirely (postponing). Smoothing = emphasizing areas of agreement and downplaying differences while yielding to the other party. Withdrawing avoids the issue; smoothing addresses it superficially.

---

**Q:** According to PMI, what is the #1 source of conflict in projects?

**A:** Schedules — disagreements about timelines, deadlines, and sequencing. (Note: Personality conflicts are LAST on the list.)

---

**Q:** What should a PM do when conflict arises during the Storming stage?

**A:** Facilitate productive conflict resolution. Normalize the disagreement ("this is healthy"), use collaborative techniques, focus on issues not personalities, and guide the team through it. Do NOT suppress the conflict.

---

**Q:** Should conflict be addressed publicly or privately?

**A:** Privately. Address conflict in a private setting to protect psychological safety. Public confrontation damages trust and team dynamics.

## Team Development (Tuckman's Model)

**Q:** What are the 5 stages of Tuckman's model in order?

**A:** Forming → Storming → Norming → Performing → Adjourning (Mourning).

---

**Q:** During which Tuckman stage is conflict highest?

**A:** Storming — this is when team members challenge each other, compete for roles, and push back against authority. It's normal and necessary.

---

**Q:** What should the PM do during the Forming stage?

**A:** Provide clear direction and structure, define roles and responsibilities, establish ground rules and team charter, create opportunities for team bonding, and be more directive about process.

---

**Q:** What should the PM do during the Performing stage?

**A:** Get out of the way. Delegate extensively, remove external obstacles, provide resources, protect the team from interference, and celebrate successes. Do NOT micromanage.

---

**Q:** What causes a team to regress to an earlier Tuckman stage?

**A:** Adding or losing team members, major scope changes, organizational restructuring, new PM taking over, or significant unresolved conflict. The team must re-form and re-norm.

---

**Q:** Do all teams reach the Performing stage?

**A:** No. Teams must successfully navigate Storming and Norming first. Some teams get stuck in Storming or never develop the trust needed for Performing.

---

**Q:** What is psychological safety and why does it matter?

**A:** The belief that you won't be punished for speaking up with ideas, questions, concerns, or mistakes. It enables risk identification, innovation, constructive conflict, and high performance.

---

**Q:** Who should create the team charter?

**A:** The team creates it collaboratively, facilitated by the PM. It should NOT be created by the PM alone and imposed on the team.

---

**Q:** What extra challenges do virtual teams face in team development?

**A:** Reduced informal communication, time zone differences, cultural differences, harder to build trust, isolation, and misinterpretation of written communication. Virtual teams take LONGER to progress through Tuckman's stages.

## Motivation Theories

**Q:** What are the 5 levels of Maslow's hierarchy from bottom to top?

**A:** 1) Physiological (salary, basic conditions), 2) Safety (job security, stability), 3) Belonging/Social (team, friendship), 4) Esteem (recognition, respect), 5) Self-Actualization (reaching full potential).

---

**Q:** According to Maslow, can you motivate someone with recognition if they're worried about job security?

**A:** No. Lower-level needs must be satisfied before higher-level needs become motivating. Address the safety concern first, then recognition becomes meaningful.

---

**Q:** What is Herzberg's key insight about money/salary?

**A:** Money is a HYGIENE FACTOR, not a motivator. Its absence causes dissatisfaction, but its presence doesn't create motivation. To truly motivate, use motivators: meaningful work, recognition, growth, achievement.

---

**Q:** Name 3 Herzberg motivators and 3 hygiene factors.

**A:** Motivators: Achievement, recognition, the work itself, responsibility, growth, advancement. Hygiene: Salary, job security, working conditions, company policies, supervision, relationships.

---

**Q:** What is the difference between Theory X and Theory Y?

**A:** Theory X assumes people are lazy, dislike work, and need control/threats. Theory Y assumes people are self-motivated, enjoy work, and seek responsibility. PMI aligns with Theory Y.

---

**Q:** What are McClelland's three acquired needs?

**A:** Achievement (nAch) — drive to excel; Affiliation (nAff) — drive for relationships; Power (nPow) — drive to influence and lead.

---

**Q:** What is Vroom's Expectancy Theory formula?

**A:** Motivation = Expectancy × Instrumentality × Valence. (Can I do it? × Will I be rewarded? × Do I want the reward?) If ANY factor is zero, motivation is zero.

---

**Q:** A team member says "Why bother trying? Nobody notices good work here." Which Vroom component is broken?

**A:** Instrumentality — they don't believe performance will lead to a reward. The PM should create visible recognition systems and follow through on promises.

---

**Q:** A team member says "This deadline is impossible, so why try?" Which Vroom component is broken?

**A:** Expectancy — they don't believe effort will lead to successful performance. The PM should break the work down, provide support, or adjust expectations to be achievable.

---

**Q:** Does PMI prefer intrinsic or extrinsic motivation?

**A:** Intrinsic — meaningful work, autonomy, mastery, and purpose. Extrinsic motivators (bonuses, pizza parties) are short-term. PMI wants sustainable, internal motivation.

## Stakeholder Engagement

**Q:** What are the four quadrants of the Power/Interest grid?

**A:** Manage Closely (High Power/High Interest), Keep Satisfied (High Power/Low Interest), Keep Informed (Low Power/High Interest), Monitor (Low Power/Low Interest).

---

**Q:** What strategy should you use for a stakeholder with high power and high interest?

**A:** Manage Closely — active engagement, involve in decisions, frequent face-to-face communication, build strong personal relationships, provide detailed progress reports.

---

**Q:** What are the 5 stakeholder engagement levels?

**A:** Unaware → Resistant → Neutral → Supportive → Leading.

---

**Q:** What should you do FIRST when you encounter a resistant stakeholder?

**A:** Understand their concerns — listen actively, identify the root cause of resistance (fear of change? loss of power? legitimate concern?). You must understand before you can address.

---

**Q:** What are the three attributes in the Salience model?

**A:** Power (ability to impose will), Legitimacy (appropriateness of involvement), and Urgency (time-sensitivity of claims).

---

**Q:** What is a "Definitive" stakeholder in the Salience model?

**A:** A stakeholder with ALL THREE attributes: Power + Legitimacy + Urgency. They require immediate PM attention and are the highest priority.

---

**Q:** Is stakeholder identification a one-time activity?

**A:** No. It's ONGOING throughout the project. New stakeholders can emerge at any phase and existing stakeholders can change in power, interest, or engagement level.

---

**Q:** What type of communication does PMI prefer for important stakeholder interactions?

**A:** Interactive (two-way) communication — meetings, calls, face-to-face. Not emails or reports for sensitive or important discussions.

---

**Q:** Can stakeholders move between quadrants on the Power/Interest grid?

**A:** Yes. Stakeholders can gain or lose power (promotion, reorganization) or interest (scope changes affect them). Reassess regularly and adjust strategy accordingly.

## Emotional Intelligence

**Q:** What are the 5 components of Emotional Intelligence (Goleman's model)?

**A:** 1) Self-Awareness, 2) Self-Regulation, 3) Motivation, 4) Empathy, 5) Social Skills.

---

**Q:** Which EQ component is the foundation for all others?

**A:** Self-Awareness — you must recognize your own emotions before you can manage them or understand others'.

---

**Q:** What is self-regulation in the context of EQ?

**A:** The ability to control or redirect disruptive emotions and impulses; thinking before acting. Staying calm under pressure, not making decisions when angry, adapting without overreacting.

---

**Q:** Is empathy the same as agreement?

**A:** No. Empathy means understanding another person's perspective and feelings. You can empathize with a stakeholder's concern while still proceeding with your decision. Understanding ≠ agreeing.

---

**Q:** What should a PM do FIRST when they receive an angry, accusatory email?

**A:** Pause and manage their emotional reaction (self-regulation). Do NOT reply immediately while feeling defensive. Then respond calmly with facts and an offer to discuss.

---

**Q:** For leadership roles, is EQ or IQ more predictive of success?

**A:** EQ. Research shows emotional intelligence is more predictive of leadership success than cognitive intelligence. PMI values people skills over technical skills for PMs.

---

**Q:** How does a PM build psychological safety using EQ?

**A:** Admit own mistakes (self-awareness), respond calmly to bad news (self-regulation), show genuine interest in team concerns (empathy), and create an environment where speaking up is rewarded, not punished (social skills).

---

**Q:** A usually high-performing team member has become quiet and is missing deadlines. What EQ component should the PM apply first?

**A:** Empathy — have a private, caring conversation to understand what's happening. A sudden behavior change signals something is wrong. Listen before problem-solving.

## Mixed/Integration Questions

**Q:** A new team member joins a high-performing agile team. What should the PM expect?

**A:** The team will temporarily regress (likely to Forming). The PM should facilitate introductions, help the new member understand team norms, and allow time for the team to re-establish its rhythm.

---

**Q:** Your team is in the Storming stage and two members are in heated conflict about the technical approach. What conflict resolution technique should you use?

**A:** Collaborate/Problem-Solve — facilitate a discussion where both present their perspectives, identify underlying interests, and work toward a solution that addresses both parties' concerns.

---

**Q:** A team member is technically brilliant but alienates colleagues with harsh criticism. What's the root issue?

**A:** Low Emotional Intelligence — specifically low empathy and poor social skills. The PM should coach them on how their communication style affects others and help them develop more constructive feedback approaches.

---

**Q:** According to PMI, what should a PM do BEFORE trying to motivate a team member who seems disengaged?

**A:** Understand WHY they're disengaged. Use empathy to have a conversation, identify which need is unmet (Maslow), which Vroom component is broken, or what hygiene factor is missing (Herzberg). Then address the root cause.

---

**Q:** A stakeholder who was previously in the "Monitor" quadrant just got promoted to VP and now has authority over your project's budget. What should you do?

**A:** Re-classify them to "Keep Satisfied" or "Manage Closely" (depending on their interest level), update your stakeholder engagement strategy, and proactively build a relationship with them before issues arise.

---

**Q:** What is the relationship between servant leadership and emotional intelligence?

**A:** Servant leadership REQUIRES high EQ. You need self-awareness (to know your impact), self-regulation (to stay calm), empathy (to understand team needs), and social skills (to facilitate and influence). You can't serve what you don't understand.
