# Stakeholder Engagement & Management

## Overview
Stakeholder management is a critical People domain topic. PMI views stakeholder engagement as **proactive and ongoing** — not a one-time activity. You need to identify stakeholders, assess their influence and interest, plan engagement strategies, and continuously monitor and adjust. This chapter covers all the tools and techniques you need for the exam.

---

## Stakeholder Identification

### Who Is a Stakeholder?
A stakeholder is **anyone who may affect, be affected by, or perceive themselves to be affected by** a project decision, activity, or outcome.

### Common Stakeholders

| Category | Examples |
|----------|----------|
| **Internal** | Project team, sponsor, PMO, functional managers, executives |
| **External** | Customers, end users, vendors, regulators, community |
| **Positive** | Those who benefit from the project (supporters) |
| **Negative** | Those who may be harmed or oppose the project (resistors) |

### Identification Techniques

| Technique | Description | When to Use |
|-----------|-------------|-------------|
| **Brainstorming** | Team generates list of all possible stakeholders | Initial identification |
| **Expert judgment** | Consult experienced PMs or subject matter experts | Complex stakeholder landscapes |
| **Stakeholder mapping** | Visual representation of stakeholders and relationships | Understanding connections |
| **Document analysis** | Review contracts, org charts, prior project files | Formal identification |
| **Questionnaires/surveys** | Ask known stakeholders who else should be involved | Expanding the list |
| **Meetings** | Facilitated sessions to identify and classify | Collaborative identification |

🎯 **Exam Tip:** Stakeholder identification is an ONGOING activity, not just a kickoff task. New stakeholders can emerge at any phase. If a question describes a new stakeholder appearing mid-project, the correct answer involves adding them to the stakeholder register and assessing their engagement needs.

## Power/Interest Grid (The Most Tested Tool)

The Power/Interest grid classifies stakeholders into four quadrants based on their level of authority (power) and their level of concern (interest) about the project.

```
High Power │                    │
           │   KEEP SATISFIED   │   MANAGE CLOSELY
           │   (Monitor)        │   (Active engagement)
           │                    │
           │────────────────────│────────────────────
           │                    │
           │   MONITOR          │   KEEP INFORMED
           │   (Minimal effort) │   (Communication)
           │                    │
Low Power  │                    │
           └────────────────────┴────────────────────
           Low Interest              High Interest
```

### Quadrant Strategies

| Quadrant | Power | Interest | Strategy | Communication |
|----------|-------|----------|----------|---------------|
| **Manage Closely** | High | High | Active engagement, involve in decisions | Frequent, detailed, two-way |
| **Keep Satisfied** | High | Low | Keep happy, don't bore with details | Regular updates, high-level |
| **Keep Informed** | Low | High | Provide information, address concerns | Regular, detailed, one-way |
| **Monitor** | Low | Low | Minimal effort, watch for changes | Periodic, minimal |

### Detailed Strategies by Quadrant

#### Manage Closely (High Power, High Interest)
- **Who:** Project sponsor, key customer, executive champion
- **Strategy:** These are your most important stakeholders
- **Actions:**
  - Involve in key decisions
  - Regular face-to-face meetings
  - Seek their input proactively
  - Manage their expectations carefully
  - Build strong personal relationships
  - Provide detailed progress reports

#### Keep Satisfied (High Power, Low Interest)
- **Who:** Senior executives, board members, regulatory bodies
- **Strategy:** Don't annoy them; keep them happy without overwhelming
- **Actions:**
  - Provide high-level status updates
  - Alert them to major issues or decisions
  - Don't flood with details
  - Understand what triggers their interest
  - Be prepared if they suddenly become interested

#### Keep Informed (Low Power, High Interest)
- **Who:** End users, team members' functional managers, community groups
- **Strategy:** Keep them in the loop; they can become advocates or resistors
- **Actions:**
  - Regular newsletters or status reports
  - Invite to demos and reviews
  - Address their concerns
  - Leverage them as champions
  - They may gain power over time — watch for changes

#### Monitor (Low Power, Low Interest)
- **Who:** Peripheral departments, distant stakeholders
- **Strategy:** Minimal effort but don't ignore completely
- **Actions:**
  - Periodic check-ins
  - Watch for changes in power or interest
  - Include in broad communications
  - Be ready to re-classify if situation changes

🎯 **Exam Tip:** Stakeholders can MOVE between quadrants. A "Monitor" stakeholder might gain power (promotion) or interest (scope change affects them). Reassess regularly. If a question describes a stakeholder whose situation has changed, the answer involves re-classifying and adjusting strategy.

## Salience Model

The Salience model classifies stakeholders based on THREE attributes:

| Attribute | Definition |
|-----------|------------|
| **Power** | Ability to impose their will on the project |
| **Legitimacy** | Appropriateness of their involvement |
| **Urgency** | Time-sensitivity of their claims |

### Stakeholder Classes

| Class | Attributes | Priority | Example |
|-------|-----------|----------|--------|
| **Definitive** | Power + Legitimacy + Urgency | Highest — immediate attention | Sponsor demanding a scope change NOW |
| **Dominant** | Power + Legitimacy | High — significant influence | Executive with authority over the project |
| **Dangerous** | Power + Urgency | High — can disrupt | Union threatening a strike |
| **Dependent** | Legitimacy + Urgency | Medium — need an advocate | End users with urgent needs but no power |
| **Dormant** | Power only | Low (currently) — watch | Executive who could intervene but hasn't |
| **Discretionary** | Legitimacy only | Low — no pressure | Community group with valid interest |
| **Demanding** | Urgency only | Low — noisy but powerless | Vocal critic with no authority |

🎯 **Exam Tip:** The Salience model is more nuanced than the Power/Interest grid. Use it when a question involves stakeholders with urgency as a factor. "Definitive" stakeholders (all three attributes) require immediate PM attention.

## Stakeholder Engagement Levels

PMI defines five levels of stakeholder engagement:

| Level | Description | Stakeholder Behavior |
|-------|-------------|---------------------|
| **Unaware** | Doesn't know about the project | No engagement at all |
| **Resistant** | Aware but opposed to the project | Active opposition, blocking |
| **Neutral** | Aware but neither supportive nor resistant | Indifferent, won't help or hinder |
| **Supportive** | Aware and supportive of the project | Willing to help, positive attitude |
| **Leading** | Actively engaged and driving success | Champions the project, recruits others |

### Engagement Assessment Matrix

The engagement assessment matrix tracks CURRENT vs. DESIRED engagement for each stakeholder:

| Stakeholder | Unaware | Resistant | Neutral | Supportive | Leading |
|-------------|---------|-----------|---------|------------|--------|
| Sponsor | | | | | C/D |
| VP Engineering | | | C | D | |
| End Users | | C | | D | |
| Regulatory Body | | | C/D | | |
| Team Lead | | | | C | D |

**C** = Current engagement level
**D** = Desired engagement level

### Moving Stakeholders Along the Spectrum

| Current → Desired | Strategy |
|-------------------|----------|
| Unaware → Neutral/Supportive | Inform, educate, show benefits |
| Resistant → Neutral | Understand concerns, address root causes, involve in decisions |
| Resistant → Supportive | Address concerns, show WIIFM (What's In It For Me), give ownership |
| Neutral → Supportive | Demonstrate value, involve in project, show impact |
| Supportive → Leading | Empower, give visible role, leverage their influence |

🎯 **Exam Tip:** You don't always need to move stakeholders to "Leading." The goal is to get them to the DESIRED level. Sometimes "Neutral" is perfectly acceptable for a peripheral stakeholder. Focus energy on stakeholders where the gap between Current and Desired is largest AND who have the most impact.

## Communication Strategies by Stakeholder Type

### Communication Methods

| Method | Richness | Best For |
|--------|----------|----------|
| **Face-to-face** | Highest | Sensitive issues, conflict, relationship building |
| **Video call** | High | Remote teams, visual communication |
| **Phone call** | Medium-High | Quick decisions, personal touch |
| **Email** | Medium | Documentation, non-urgent updates |
| **Written report** | Medium | Formal status, audit trail |
| **Dashboard** | Low-Medium | At-a-glance status for many stakeholders |
| **Newsletter** | Low | Broad updates to many stakeholders |

### Matching Communication to Stakeholder Needs

| Stakeholder Type | Frequency | Detail Level | Method | Direction |
|-----------------|-----------|--------------|--------|----------|
| Manage Closely | Daily/Weekly | High | Face-to-face, video | Two-way (interactive) |
| Keep Satisfied | Monthly/Milestone | Executive summary | Email, brief meeting | Push (you initiate) |
| Keep Informed | Weekly/Bi-weekly | Moderate | Email, newsletter, demo | Push (you initiate) |
| Monitor | Quarterly/As needed | Minimal | Dashboard, newsletter | Pull (they access if needed) |

### Communication Models

**Push Communication:** PM sends information to stakeholders (emails, reports)
- Best for: Keep Informed, Keep Satisfied
- Risk: Information overload, may not be read

**Pull Communication:** Stakeholders access information when they want it (dashboards, wikis)
- Best for: Monitor, large stakeholder groups
- Risk: Stakeholders may not check

**Interactive Communication:** Two-way exchange (meetings, calls, workshops)
- Best for: Manage Closely, conflict resolution, decision-making
- Risk: Time-intensive, scheduling challenges

🎯 **Exam Tip:** PMI prefers INTERACTIVE communication for important stakeholders and sensitive issues. If a question involves a conflict or misunderstanding with a key stakeholder, the answer is almost always "meet with them" — not "send an email."

## Stakeholder Engagement Strategies

### For Resistant Stakeholders
1. **Understand their concerns** — listen actively, don't dismiss
2. **Identify root cause** — is it fear of change? Loss of power? Legitimate concern?
3. **Address concerns directly** — show how you'll mitigate their risks
4. **Involve them** — give them a role or voice in decisions
5. **Show WIIFM** — demonstrate personal benefit
6. **Find common ground** — align project goals with their goals
7. **Use their allies** — leverage relationships with people they trust

### For Unaware Stakeholders
1. **Inform early** — don't surprise them later
2. **Explain impact** — how the project affects them
3. **Provide context** — why the project exists
4. **Offer involvement** — give them a way to participate
5. **Follow up** — ensure the message was received

### For Supportive Stakeholders (Leverage Them)
1. **Empower as champions** — let them advocate for the project
2. **Give visible roles** — steering committee, advisory board
3. **Keep them informed** — maintain their support with updates
4. **Ask for help** — leverage their influence with resistant stakeholders
5. **Recognize their support** — acknowledge their contribution

## Practice Scenarios

### Scenario 1
**A senior VP (high power) has shown no interest in your project until now. Suddenly, a scope change will affect their department. They're now asking pointed questions and seem concerned. What should you do?**

- ❌ A) Continue with your current communication plan — they're in the "Keep Satisfied" quadrant
- ✅ B) Re-classify them to "Manage Closely" and schedule a dedicated meeting to address their concerns
- ❌ C) Send them a detailed status report
- ❌ D) Ask your sponsor to handle the relationship

**Why B?** Their interest level has changed. They've moved from "Keep Satisfied" to "Manage Closely." Adjust your strategy accordingly with interactive communication.

### Scenario 2
**End users are resistant to the new system your project is implementing. They've been vocal about preferring the old system. What should the PM do FIRST?**

- ❌ A) Proceed with implementation — resistance is normal
- ❌ B) Escalate to the sponsor to mandate adoption
- ✅ C) Meet with end user representatives to understand their specific concerns
- ❌ D) Create a training plan to help them learn the new system

**Why C?** Before you can address resistance, you must UNDERSTAND it. Meet with them (interactive communication), listen to concerns, then develop a targeted strategy.

### Scenario 3
**Your project sponsor is supportive but you want them to be "Leading" — actively championing the project to other executives. What should you do?**

- ❌ A) Ask them directly to promote the project
- ✅ B) Provide them with success stories, data, and talking points they can share with peers
- ❌ C) CC them on all project communications
- ❌ D) Invite them to every team meeting

**Why B?** To move someone from Supportive to Leading, empower them with the tools to advocate. Make it easy for them to champion the project.

### Scenario 4
**You discover a regulatory body that should have been identified as a stakeholder at project initiation. You're now in the execution phase. What should you do?**

- ❌ A) Document the oversight in lessons learned for the next project
- ❌ B) Continue without engaging them — it's too late to add stakeholders
- ✅ C) Add them to the stakeholder register, assess their engagement needs, and develop an appropriate strategy
- ❌ D) Escalate to the sponsor for guidance

**Why C?** Stakeholder identification is ongoing. New stakeholders can be identified at any time. Add them, assess them, engage them.

## Key Takeaways for the Exam

1. **Stakeholder identification is ONGOING** — not just a kickoff activity
2. **Power/Interest grid** is the most commonly tested classification tool
3. **Stakeholders can move between quadrants** — reassess regularly
4. **Resistant stakeholders need understanding first** — listen before acting
5. **Interactive communication** for important/sensitive stakeholder interactions
6. **The engagement assessment matrix** tracks current vs. desired levels
7. **You don't need everyone at "Leading"** — match effort to impact
8. **Proactive engagement prevents problems** — don't wait for stakeholders to come to you

🎯 **Final Exam Tip:** When a stakeholder question appears on the exam, first classify the stakeholder (power, interest, engagement level), then choose the strategy appropriate for that classification. The most common wrong answer is treating all stakeholders the same way.
