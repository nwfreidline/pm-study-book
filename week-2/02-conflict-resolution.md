# Conflict Resolution: Thomas-Kilmann Model

## Overview
Conflict is inevitable in projects. PMI doesn't want you to avoid conflict — they want you to **manage it effectively**. The Thomas-Kilmann Conflict Mode Instrument (TKI) defines 5 approaches to conflict, and PMI has a clear preference order. This is one of the most heavily tested topics in the People domain.

---

## The Thomas-Kilmann Model

The model maps conflict approaches on two axes:
- **Assertiveness** (concern for your own needs/goals)
- **Cooperativeness** (concern for the other party's needs/goals)

```
High Assertiveness
        │
   Competing ─────────── Collaborating
        │                      │
        │      Compromising    │
        │           ●          │
        │                      │
   Avoiding ─────────── Accommodating
        │
Low Assertiveness
        
   Low Cooperativeness ──── High Cooperativeness
```

## The 5 Conflict Resolution Techniques

### 1. Collaborate / Problem-Solve (Win-Win) ⭐ PMI's #1 PREFERENCE

| Aspect | Detail |
|--------|--------|
| **Assertiveness** | High |
| **Cooperativeness** | High |
| **Definition** | Work together to find a solution that fully satisfies all parties |
| **PMI Name** | Collaborate/Problem-Solve |
| **Outcome** | Win-Win |
| **Time Required** | High |
| **Relationship Impact** | Strengthens relationships |

**When to Use:**
- When the issue is important to both parties
- When you have time to work through the problem
- When you need a long-term solution
- When maintaining the relationship matters
- When creative solutions are possible

**PM Behaviors:**
- Facilitate open discussion
- Identify underlying interests (not just positions)
- Brainstorm multiple solutions
- Find integrative solutions that address all concerns

**Example Scenario:**
Two team members disagree on the technical architecture. The PM facilitates a meeting where both present their approaches, identifies the strengths of each, and helps the team design a hybrid solution that incorporates the best elements of both proposals.

🎯 **Exam Tip:** This is PMI's PREFERRED approach. If "collaborate" or "problem-solve" appears as an answer choice and the scenario doesn't describe a crisis or trivial issue, it's almost always correct.

---

### 2. Compromise / Reconcile (Lose-Lose)

| Aspect | Detail |
|--------|--------|
| **Assertiveness** | Medium |
| **Cooperativeness** | Medium |
| **Definition** | Each party gives up something to reach a middle ground |
| **PMI Name** | Compromise/Reconcile |
| **Outcome** | Lose-Lose (both give up something) |
| **Time Required** | Medium |
| **Relationship Impact** | Neutral |

**When to Use:**
- When both parties have equal power
- When you need a temporary solution quickly
- When collaboration has failed
- When the issue is moderately important
- When a deadline is approaching and you need resolution

**PM Behaviors:**
- Identify what each party can give up
- Find the middle ground
- Ensure both parties feel the trade-off is fair
- Document the agreement

**Example Scenario:**
The development team wants 3 sprints for a feature; the Product Owner wants it in 1 sprint. The PM facilitates a compromise: deliver core functionality in 2 sprints with enhancements in a later release.

🎯 **Exam Tip:** Compromise sounds reasonable but PMI considers it "lose-lose" because nobody gets everything they want. It's acceptable when collaboration isn't possible, but it's NOT the first choice.

---

### 3. Smooth / Accommodate (Lose-Win)

| Aspect | Detail |
|--------|--------|
| **Assertiveness** | Low |
| **Cooperativeness** | High |
| **Definition** | Emphasize areas of agreement, downplay differences, yield to the other party |
| **PMI Name** | Smooth/Accommodate |
| **Outcome** | You Lose - They Win |
| **Time Required** | Low |
| **Relationship Impact** | Preserves harmony (short-term) |

**When to Use:**
- When the issue is more important to the other party
- When preserving the relationship is more important than winning
- When you realize you're wrong
- When continued conflict would damage the team
- When you want to build goodwill for a future issue

**PM Behaviors:**
- Acknowledge the other party's concerns
- Emphasize common ground
- Yield gracefully
- Don't keep score

**Example Scenario:**
A senior stakeholder insists on a specific reporting format that differs from your preference. Since the content is the same and the format is a minor issue, you accommodate their preference to maintain the relationship for more important negotiations later.

🎯 **Exam Tip:** Smoothing/accommodating is appropriate for minor issues where the relationship matters more than the outcome. It's NOT appropriate for important technical or safety decisions.

---

### 4. Force / Direct (Win-Lose)

| Aspect | Detail |
|--------|--------|
| **Assertiveness** | High |
| **Cooperativeness** | Low |
| **Definition** | Push your viewpoint at the expense of others; use authority |
| **PMI Name** | Force/Direct |
| **Outcome** | You Win - They Lose |
| **Time Required** | Low |
| **Relationship Impact** | Damages relationships |

**When to Use:**
- Emergency situations requiring immediate action
- When safety is at risk
- When you know you're right and the issue is critical
- When unpopular but necessary decisions must be made
- As a last resort when all other approaches have failed

**PM Behaviors:**
- Make the decision
- Communicate clearly
- Accept responsibility for the outcome
- Use sparingly

**Example Scenario:**
During a production deployment, two team members argue about the rollback procedure while the system is down. The PM makes the call: "We're rolling back now using Procedure A. We'll debrief tomorrow."

🎯 **Exam Tip:** Forcing is almost NEVER the correct answer on the PMP exam unless the scenario explicitly describes an emergency, safety issue, or situation where all collaborative approaches have been exhausted.

---

### 5. Withdraw / Avoid (Lose-Lose)

| Aspect | Detail |
|--------|--------|
| **Assertiveness** | Low |
| **Cooperativeness** | Low |
| **Definition** | Retreat from the conflict; postpone the issue |
| **PMI Name** | Withdraw/Avoid |
| **Outcome** | Lose-Lose (issue unresolved) |
| **Time Required** | None (deferred) |
| **Relationship Impact** | Can worsen over time |

**When to Use:**
- When the issue is trivial
- When you need a cooling-off period
- When you have no chance of winning
- When the potential damage of confrontation outweighs the benefit
- When more information is needed before addressing the issue

**PM Behaviors:**
- Postpone the discussion
- Change the subject
- Step away temporarily
- Revisit when emotions have cooled

**Example Scenario:**
Two team members get into a heated argument during a meeting. Emotions are running high and neither is thinking clearly. The PM says, "Let's table this for now and revisit it tomorrow morning when we've all had time to think."

🎯 **Exam Tip:** Withdrawal is a TEMPORARY measure. If an answer says "avoid the conflict" without any plan to address it later, it's wrong. Temporary withdrawal to let emotions cool is acceptable.

## PMI's Preference Order

| Rank | Technique | When PMI Wants It |
|------|-----------|-------------------|
| 1st | **Collaborate/Problem-Solve** | Default — try this first, always |
| 2nd | **Compromise/Reconcile** | When collaboration fails or time is limited |
| 3rd | **Smooth/Accommodate** | Minor issues, relationship preservation |
| 4th | **Force/Direct** | Emergencies, safety, last resort |
| 5th | **Withdraw/Avoid** | Cooling off only — must revisit later |

🎯 **Exam Tip:** Memorize this order. When a question asks "what should the PM do FIRST?" about a conflict, the answer is almost always collaborate/problem-solve. The only exception is if the scenario describes an emergency (force) or extremely heated emotions (temporary withdrawal).

## Sources of Conflict in Projects

PMI identifies these common sources (in approximate order of frequency):

| Rank | Source | Description |
|------|--------|-------------|
| 1 | **Schedules** | Disagreements about timelines, deadlines, sequencing |
| 2 | **Priorities** | Competing demands for resources or attention |
| 3 | **Resources** | Not enough people, equipment, or budget |
| 4 | **Technical opinions** | Disagreements about approach or architecture |
| 5 | **Administrative procedures** | Process disagreements |
| 6 | **Cost** | Budget allocation conflicts |
| 7 | **Personality** | Interpersonal friction |

🎯 **Exam Tip:** Notice that PERSONALITY is LAST on the list. PMI believes most conflicts are about work issues (schedules, priorities, resources), not personal issues. If an exam question attributes conflict to personality, look for the underlying work-related cause.

## The PM's Role in Conflict

### What PMI Expects
1. **Address conflict early** — don't let it fester
2. **Facilitate, don't dictate** — help parties find their own solution
3. **Focus on issues, not people** — separate the problem from the person
4. **Look for root causes** — address underlying interests, not surface positions
5. **Create a safe environment** — people should feel comfortable raising concerns
6. **Use private settings** — address conflict privately, not in front of the whole team
7. **Document agreements** — ensure resolution sticks

### What PMI Does NOT Want
- ❌ Ignoring conflict and hoping it goes away
- ❌ Taking sides
- ❌ Using authority to force a resolution (except emergencies)
- ❌ Escalating to management as a first step
- ❌ Blaming individuals
- ❌ Addressing conflict publicly to "make an example"

## Conflict and Team Development

Conflict behavior changes across Tuckman's stages:

| Stage | Conflict Level | PM Response |
|-------|---------------|-------------|
| Forming | Low (people are polite) | Establish ground rules for future conflict |
| **Storming** | **HIGH** — this is where conflict peaks | Facilitate, normalize disagreement, guide toward collaboration |
| Norming | Decreasing — team finds its rhythm | Reinforce collaborative behaviors |
| Performing | Low — handled by team itself | Step back, let team self-manage conflicts |
| Adjourning | Variable — anxiety about ending | Acknowledge emotions, celebrate achievements |

🎯 **Exam Tip:** Conflict during Storming is NORMAL and HEALTHY. PMI does NOT want you to suppress it. The correct response is to facilitate productive conflict resolution, not to avoid or force through it.

## Practice Scenarios

### Scenario 1
**Two developers disagree about whether to use REST or GraphQL for the API. Both have valid technical arguments. The decision needs to be made this sprint. What should the PM do?**

- ❌ A) Make the decision based on your own technical preference
- ❌ B) Flip a coin to be fair
- ✅ C) Facilitate a discussion where both present pros/cons, then guide the team to a consensus decision based on project requirements
- ❌ D) Escalate to the technical architect

**Why C?** Collaborate/problem-solve. The PM facilitates, the team decides based on merit.

### Scenario 2
**A stakeholder and the Product Owner disagree about feature priority. The stakeholder wants Feature A first; the PO wants Feature B. Both features are in the backlog. What should the PM do?**

- ❌ A) Side with the Product Owner since they own the backlog
- ✅ B) Facilitate a meeting to understand the business value of each and help them reach agreement
- ❌ C) Compromise by doing half of each feature
- ❌ D) Escalate to the project sponsor

**Why B?** Collaborate first. Help them understand each other's reasoning and find alignment.

### Scenario 3
**During a retrospective, a team member angrily accuses another of not pulling their weight. The accused team member looks embarrassed and the room goes silent. What should the PM do FIRST?**

- ❌ A) Ask the accuser to provide specific examples
- ✅ B) Acknowledge the concern, redirect to a private conversation after the meeting
- ❌ C) Tell the accuser their behavior is inappropriate
- ❌ D) Move on to the next retrospective topic

**Why B?** The immediate need is to de-escalate (temporary smoothing/withdrawal from public setting), then address privately through collaboration. Public confrontation damages psychological safety.

### Scenario 4
**A vendor and your team disagree about the interpretation of a contract clause. The vendor insists their reading is correct and threatens to stop work. What should the PM do?**

- ❌ A) Threaten legal action
- ❌ B) Accept the vendor's interpretation to keep work moving
- ✅ C) Arrange a meeting with both parties and the contract documentation to collaboratively resolve the ambiguity
- ❌ D) Escalate immediately to legal

**Why C?** Collaborate first. Bring facts (the contract) and work together to find the correct interpretation. Escalation to legal is premature.

---

## Key Takeaways for the Exam

1. **Collaborate/Problem-Solve is ALWAYS the first choice** unless there's a specific reason it won't work
2. **Compromise is NOT collaboration** — it's a fallback when collaboration fails
3. **Forcing is for emergencies only** — never the default
4. **Withdrawal must be temporary** — always plan to revisit
5. **Most conflict is about work, not personality** — look for root causes
6. **Address conflict early and privately** — don't let it fester or go public
7. **Conflict during Storming is normal** — facilitate it, don't suppress it
8. **The PM facilitates resolution** — doesn't dictate the answer

🎯 **Final Exam Tip:** If you're stuck between "collaborate" and any other answer, choose collaborate. PMI's exam is designed to reward collaborative, team-empowering approaches to conflict.
